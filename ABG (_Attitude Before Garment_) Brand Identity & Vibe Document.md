# **ABG ("Attitude Before Garment") Brand Identity & Vibe Document**

## **1\. The Core Identity**

*This section defines who you are, what you stand for, and the cultural flip you are executing.*

* **Brand Name:** ABG  
* **The Meaning:** Attitude Before Garment. Not Asian Baby Girl, not "Acoustic But Goated" anymore — the attitude comes first, the clothes are just where it happens to land. You don't put on an ABG piece to build a personality. You already have one; the piece just doesn't get in the way of it.  
* **The Elevator Pitch:** Most clothing is trying to tell you something — a logo saying "look how much this cost," a graphic saying "look at me." ABG isn't trying to say anything. It's for people who already know what they like and would rather not explain it. What we actually obsess over is the cut — pattern, proportion, how a piece sits and moves — not the print. If we make a t-shirt, it's not a canvas for a graphic; it's a genuinely well-cut t-shirt, full stop. Premium construction, considered fit, no gatekept price tag. We also built our own checkout tech, so when a drop goes live, it's clean — no chasing, no chaos.  
* **The Mission:** To make clothing for people who are already improving at the things they care about — quietly, without turning the process into content. The craft investment goes into cut and construction first. Restrained design, honest pricing. The garment should speak through how it's made, not what's printed on it.

## **2\. The Aesthetic Collision**

*This section defines the visual tension of the brand. You are combining two very different instincts — cult-favorite basics and a point of view that refuses to be loud about itself.*

* **The Foundation (The "Brandy Melville" Element):**  
  * **Vibe:** Effortless, cult-favorite, minimalist, highly wearable everyday basics.  
  * **Silhouettes:** Long Baby Tee, Oversized Tee, Heavy Hoodie — each engineered around fit, not just sized up or down from a block.  
* **The Edge (Revised — the cut is the statement, not the print):**  
  * **Vibe:** Dry, deliberate, quietly confident. The boldness lives in proportion and construction decisions, not in graphics.  
  * **Design Details:** Precise pattern work, considered proportion play (drop shoulder, hem shape, sleeve length, drape), thick heavyweight fabrics chosen to hold a cut properly. Prints, if used at all, are a minor detail — never the point of the piece.  
* **The Synthesis:** "Comfortable enough that no one clocks you tried, cut well enough that someone who looks twice can tell — and you'll never confirm that to them."

**Flag:** This is a real pivot, not a tone update. Your first collection's identity (\#SamyanABG, three DTG graphics — Samyan Baddie, Samyan Bad Dih, Chamchuri Flower) was explicitly print-led. Going cut-first means those three graphics likely don't carry forward as the product logic for collection two — they were built for a print-forward brand. Worth deciding whether collection one stays as a one-off "before" chapter in your own story, or gets quietly retired from how you talk about the brand going forward.

## **3\. The "ตี๊ส" Archetype (Target Audience)**

*Working name: The ตี๊ส — could be cast as "Him" or "Her," same internal engine.*

* **Who they are:** Someone who genuinely doesn't check what's trending before deciding what they like. They formed their taste before anyone told them to, and they're mildly bored by people who are still asking. They'd rather keep growing than stay in one place.  
* **What they want, deep down:** To keep getting better at the things they actually care about — an instrument, a craft, their body, their eye for clothes. The work happens whether or not anyone's watching, and they're not managing whether it's seen. What they won't do is stage the process for an audience. The result speaks; the process isn't for sale.  
* **What they're terrified of:** Being caught trying. Not caught *not trying hard enough* — caught performing effort for an audience, or performing effortlessness for one. Both are cringe.  
* **How they move through a room:** Doesn't scan for who's watching. Doesn't lead with the phone. Ends up in the one interesting conversation in the corner rather than the center of the room.  
* **How they talk:** Dry, understated, occasionally deadpan-funny without announcing the joke. Doesn't over-explain. Would rather say one true, specific thing than five enthusiastic vague things.  
* **What's in their world:** Instagram and Pinterest — never TikTok brain-rot as the main diet. Actual books, vinyl or a real camera, a couple of pieces of clothing they've had for years next to one thing they just copped. Brands they'd actually name-drop are quiet-expensive (Rhode, COS, Loewe, vintage market finds) — never logo-forward hype brands.  
* **What would break the character:** Excitement that performs for the camera. Explaining a fit. Saying "trending" unironically.  
* **Two inflections of the same engine:**  
  * **Her:** Curated ambition, self-care, social visibility. Effortlessness is part-performed — she knows it, and admitting the effort would ruin it. Values are inward-facing: self-care, mental health, alone time.  
  * **His:** Detached mastery, taste-as-identity, doesn't perform for anyone. Genuinely non-chalant (นิ่ง) — an observer. Values are outward and analog: nature, vinyl, philosophy, deep talk — but also self-improvement and moving forward.  
* **In one line:** *People who formed their taste before anyone told them to, and are still quietly improving it without ever letting you see them try.*

## **4\. Brand Voice & Tone**

*How the brand speaks in captions, on the website, and in emails.*

* **Tone Words:** Dry, understated, specific, quietly self-aware. Confident, not chaotic.  
* **The Vibe:** Speaks like the one person in the group chat who doesn't overtype. Peer, not corporation — but a peer who'd rather say one true thing than perform enthusiasm.  
* **Copywriting Rules:**  
  * *Rule 1:* No standard marketing speak. Never say: "Shop our new high-quality collection today\!"  
  * *Rule 2:* Specific, matter-of-fact detail over vulnerability-as-spectacle. Say: "Recut the pattern twice until the hem sat right. Fixed now." Not five exclamation points about the struggle — one dry, specific fact about the actual craft.  
  * *Rule 3:* Specificity is the tool, wherever it points — fabric weight, construction choices, sample counts, real dates. Not vague hype language.  
  * *Rule 4:* Never explain the fit, never announce the joke, never say "trending" unironically. If a caption sounds like it's trying to convince someone ABG is cool, cut it.

## **5\. Visual Language & Photography**

*The strict visual rules that will make an ABG post recognizable within 0.8 seconds of scrolling.*

* **Color Palette:** \#F5F1E8, \#3F3F44  
* **Typography:** Helvetica, HK Grotesk Wide  
* **Photography Style:** Zero studio lighting, zero posing. Shot like it was taken on a real camera or iPhone, caught mid-motion rather than composed — the ตี๊ส would resent being obviously art-directed, even in their own ad. Candid over posed, considered over staged. Given the cut-first product logic, photography should let fit and drape read clearly — motion and candor, but not so blurred that the actual silhouette gets lost.

## **6\. Product & Drop Philosophy**

*How the actual physical items are treated and released.*

* **Quality Standard:** The craft investment is in the cut — pattern-making, proportion, how a piece drapes and moves — not in print technique. Moving away from cheap promotional blanks entirely. If a piece has a graphic at all, it's a minor detail on top of a genuinely well-constructed garment, never the reason the garment exists.  
* **The Drop Mechanics:** Limited runs, but urgency comes from specificity and real deadlines — not hype language. State the number made, state the date, let the scarcity be a fact rather than a pitch.

**Flag (operational, not just brand):** Cut-first product means your production dependency shifts. Collection one's constraint was DTG printer MOQs. Going forward, the real constraint becomes pattern-making and garment-factory MOQs — sampling a new cut, fitting it, and getting a factory to run it at a workable minimum is a slower, higher-skill process than reprinting a blank in a new graphic. Worth getting a cut/pattern-focused manufacturer vetted early, since it changes both your supplier tracker priorities and your timeline assumptions for collection two.
